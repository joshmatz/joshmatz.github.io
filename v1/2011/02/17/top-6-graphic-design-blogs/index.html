My Top 6 Graphic Design Blogs

Posted Feb 17 2011 by Josh Matz in Blog with 2 Comments

As a graphic designer, it’s important to stay up to date and constantly looking for sources of inspiration. I use Google Reader to do that. Google Reader pulls in posts from blogs for me automatically so I don’t have to remember each website, or click all my bookmarks to open them. When using it, it’s a good idea to organize the websites into groups (if you’re into web design and brand identity, it may be a good idea to separate the two into distinct folders/tags).

That said, these are my top 6 graphic design blogs that I follow (though, I’m currently following 23 I’ve noticed it’s nice to keep yourself from getting overwhelmed with new posts).

1. Identity Designed

Identity Designed is edited by graphic designer and author David Airey. The blog showcases brand identity and often talks about the design process the firms have gone through. ID is updated about once per week and each post includes multiple images.

Top

2. Veerle’s Blog

Veerle’s from and resides in Belgium and owns Duoh! with Geert Leyseele. Veerle often features tutorials (examples range from creating snowflakes to using the eraser tool in Illustrator). Other times she’ll go extremely in-depth with her articles on how certain things were designed (see: the design process of veerle’s blog). I’ve only linked to her Web & Graphic Design section of her blog, but if her posts are interesting enough, she also has sections on Modern Home Design and Belgian Graphic Design.

Top

3. Smashing Magazine

Founded by Vitaly Friedman, Smashing Magazine has grown a lot since 2006. And when I say a lot, I mean it. It currently receives over 5 million page views and has over 200,000 subscribers to their RSS feed. Smashing Magazine publishes a few articles every week — each being extremely in-depth — and focuses more on web design for graphic designers. That being said, there are occasional posts that are helpful to freelancers and general graphic designers (such as this one, which 25 high-quality fonts).

Top

4. GoMedia Zine

Started as a blog, GoMedia Zine has evolved into a place to find tutorials from software tools such as 3ds Max and Illustrator, to more general design categories such as Typography and Web Design. There are also other articles that involve interviews or inspiration, but their main selling point seems to be the tutorials that are posted. GoMedia Zine is updated 2-3 times per week. GoMedia also has a well designed website that’s worth checking out (and view their portfolio while you’re there, it’s also worth a look-see)!

Top

5. Abduzeedo

Abduzeedo — along with the ridiculously hard to pronounce and spell name (Portuguese for “abducted”, with a slightly altered spelling to incorporate Fabio Sasso’s company, ZEE — is my goto source for all things inspiration. You can expect at least a few posts every day that have plenty to offer in terms of content that’s been rounded up from the Internets. Inspirational articles range from photography, sculpture, illustrations, typography, and the list goes on. At it’s current count, the Daily Inspiration is at #747. At nearly about 50 images in that one article, Abduzeedo would boast over 37,000 images from which to draw inspiration. While I sometimes get bogged down by the shear load of posts published, Abduzeedo has helpful sections that would allow you to customize your reading.

Top

6. Fuel Your Creativity

Most popular article? 10 reasons why clients should have their nephew design their website. Of course, not all of Fuel Your Creativity’s articles are this whimsical (in fact, most aren’t). I like Fuel’s articles because they often times explain more of the process a designer has to go through to finish projects with clients (see this one, about handling sign-offs and revisions), which is important to me as an undergrad graphic design major who is still looking for real world experience.

Top

Whether or not you subscribe to any of these websites in a Feed Reader, as any sort of creative professional it’s important to immerse yourself in your work (and sometimes take a step back, but we can talk about that another time). I’m often recommended by professors to read certain magazines or pick up books from the library. Not everyone has the required subscription fees (especially students) for design magazines (which can be costly, though the quality is usually quite good), and many may not have good libraries to get books. I think online materials can be just as helpful and with a free price tag they’re difficult to beat.

Update: I found a way to make “bundles” in Google Reader, so if you’d like to preview or subscribe to these follow the link to my top 6 graphic design blogs bundle.
